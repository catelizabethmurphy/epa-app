Trump's history on PFAS regulation

The first Trump administration made a number of commitments to address PFAS contamination, but ultimately failed to follow through on most of them. The Biden administration finalized many of them. But the second Trump administration is now trying to roll some of them back, despite much of its rhetoric on PFAS.

January 2020
Drinking Water Protections & Hazardous Substance Designation
Congressional Action
PFAS Action Act of 2019 dies in Senate amid White House opposition

The White House — a few weeks after missing its own deadline to propose a regulatory determination for PFOA and PFOS in drinking water — publishes a statement opposing the PFAS Action Act of 2019 on procedural grounds and threatens to veto it. The statement, released just days before Congress was scheduled to vote on the January 2019 bill, acknowledges "the public concern" surrounding PFAS but argues the bill would require the administration to "bypass well-established processes, procedures, and legal requirements," of existing environmental laws, including the Safe Drinking Water Act. The bill passes the House of Representatives by a vote of 247-159, with 24 Republicans voting with most Democrats in favor of the bill, but fails to advance in the Senate.

Less than a week before leaving office, the first Trump administration puts out a pre-publication advance notice of proposed rulemaking (ANPRM) seeking public comment on whether to designate PFOA and PFOS as hazardous substances under CERCLA and the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA). January 2021
Other PFAS Actions
Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking
Trump EPA administrator issues ANPRM on potential PFAS discharge limits for chemical plants

One day after issuing an ANPRM on the potential designation of certain PFAS as regulated hazardous substances under CERCLA, Trump's EPA signs an early version of another ANPRM on potential Clean Water Act regulations limiting the amount of PFAS that manufacturers and formulators can discharge into wastewater. This would restrict the amount of PFAS that can be released into the environment to begin with, as opposed to standards under the Safe Drinking Water Act, which would limit the amount of PFAS drinking water can contain by the time it reaches consumers. Like the other ANPRM, this action is initiated in the final days of the Trump administration and leaves the actual rulemaking to the incoming administration.

2022
September 2022
Hazardous Substance Designation
Proposed Rule
Biden EPA proposes designating PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances

After freezing the Trump-era ANPRM, the Biden EPA issues a proposed rule to designate PFOA and PFOS as hazardous substances under Section 102 of CERCLA. If finalized, the designation would set a mandatory reporting threshold for accidental releases of one pound or more of PFOA and PFOS, and require them to be reported within 24 hours of the release. The rule would also give the EPA the authority to hold responsible parties accountable for cleanup and recovery costs.

2023
March 2023
Drinking Water Protections
Proposed Rule
Biden EPA proposes drinking water standards for six PFAS compounds

EPA publishes the proposed PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation, which includes legally enforceable maximum contaminant levels, or MCLS, for six PFAS compounds: PFOA, PFOS, HFPO-DA (a.k.a., GenX), PFHxS, PFNA and PFBS. The proposed limits for the two most well-known PFAS compounds, PFOA and PFOS, are set at 4 parts per trillion — the lowest reliably detectable amount and the equivalent of one drop of water in five Olympic-sized swimming pools. For context, EPA data show PFOA or PFOS at levels above 4 parts per trillion in more than 16% of water systems tested between 2023 and 2025. The EPA estimates the rule will prevent "thousands of premature deaths, tens of thousands of serious illnesses, including certain cancers and liver and heart impacts in adults, and immune and developmental impacts to infants and children."

2024
April 2024
Drinking Water Protections
Final Rule
Biden EPA finalizes first-ever binding drinking water limits for six PFAS compounds

Biden administration enacts legally enforceable drinking water limits — called maximum contaminant levels, or MCLs — for six PFAS compounds. This included defined limits for five individual PFAS compounds, including PFOA and PFOS, as well as a hazard index for mixtures of certain PFAS compounds. The EPA estimates the rule "will prevent thousands of deaths and reduce tens of thousands of serious PFAS-attributable illnesses." Per the rule's compliance deadlines, water systems must meet sampling requirements by April 2027 and must reduce PFAS levels in their drinking water to at or below the maximum contaminant levels by April 2029.

Drinking Water Protections
Court Filing
EPA files to reverse four of six PFAS drinking water standards

Trump's EPA, following through on its commitment to roll back some of the Biden-era drinking water regulations, files a motion in AWWA v. EPA asking the D.C. Circuit to partially vacate its April 2024 PFAS rule. The agency's motion seeks to rescind the standards for four of the six PFAS compounds - GenX (HFPO-DA), PFHxS, PFNA, and PFBS - while retaining the standards for PFOA and PFOS.
