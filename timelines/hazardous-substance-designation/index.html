January 2020
Congressional Action
PFAS Action Act of 2019 dies in Senate amid White House opposition

Three days before Congress is set to vote on the PFAS Action Act of 2019, the White House publishes a statement opposing the bill on procedural grounds and threatens to veto it. The statement that acknowledges "the public concern" surrounding PFAS but argues the bill would "bypass well-established processes, procedures, and legal requirements" of existing environmental laws, including CERCLA. The bill passes the House of Representatives by a vote of 247-159, with 24 Republicans voting with most Democrats in favor of the bill, but fails to advance in the Senate.

Less than a week before leaving office, the first Trump administration publishes an advance notice of proposed rulemaking (ANPRM) seeking public comment on whether to designate PFOA and PFOS as hazardous substances under CERCLA and the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA). Biden Administration 2021–2025
2022
September 2022
Proposed Rule
Biden EPA proposes designating PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances

EPA issues a proposed rule to designate PFOA and PFOS as hazardous substances under Section 102 of CERCLA. If finalized, the designation would set a mandatory reporting threshold for accidental releases of one pound or more of PFOA and PFOS, and require them to be reported within 24 hours of the release. The rule would also give the EPA the authority to hold responsible parties accountable for cleanup and recovery costs.

2024
May 2024
Final Rule
Biden EPA finalizes CERCLA hazardous substance designation for PFOA and PFOS

EPA publishes the final rule designating PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances, effective July 8, 2024. The designation triggers mandatory release reporting at one pound or more and gives EPA authority to pursue cost recovery from polluters at contaminated sites.

The U.S. Chamber of Commerce, Associated General Contractors of America and National Waste & Recycling Association file a petition for review in the D.C. Circuit challenging the CERCLA hazardous substance designation for PFOA and PFOS.

2025
September 2025
Policy Commitment
Trump II EPA announces it will retain PFOA and PFOS CERCLA designation

EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin criticizes the Biden administration for taking steps to designate PFAS as hazardous substances "without first establishing the rules of the road" and says EPA will outline a "Framework Rule" to clarify how the agency will implement the CERCLA designations and enforce liability going forward. But Zeldin - a founding member of the PFAS Congressional Taskforce and a 'strong supporter of the PFAS Action Act' - also announces the Trump administration will keep the Biden-era CERCLA designations for PFOA and PFOS and defend them in court.
