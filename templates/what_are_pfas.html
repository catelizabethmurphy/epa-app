Background

What are PFAS?

PFAS — per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — are a family of roughly 12,000 synthetic chemicals united by a single structural feature: a chain of carbon atoms bonded to fluorine. The carbon-fluorine bond is the strongest in organic chemistry. No enzyme, no bacterium, no amount of heat breaks it. Scientists engineered that permanence deliberately. It turned out to be their defining danger.

PFAS repel water, oil, and heat. They make surfaces nonstick, fabrics waterproof, and foam capable of smothering fuel fires. Those properties made them commercially irresistible. From the 1950s onward they spread into cookware, food packaging, carpets, clothing, firefighting foam, cosmetics, and semiconductor manufacturing. They are now found in the blood of virtually every American, in Arctic ice, in deep ocean fish, and in human breast milk on every inhabited continent.

A brief history

1938
Roy Plunkett, a DuPont chemist working on refrigerants in Deepwater, N.J., accidentally discovers polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) when a canister of tetrafluoroethylene gas stops flowing and he finds a white, waxy solid inside. The material has a lubricity unlike anything known — virtually nothing sticks to it. DuPont patents the compound and markets it as Teflon beginning in 1945.
1950s–1960s
PFAS proliferate across industry. 3M develops PFOS (perfluorooctane sulfonate) for Scotchgard, its stain- and water-resistant treatment for carpets and upholstery. DuPont expands Teflon manufacturing, using PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid) as a processing aid. The U.S. Navy and Air Force adopt AFFF — aqueous film-forming foam — for fuel-fire suppression at naval and air installations worldwide. The foam saturates the soil around every base that trains with it.
1998–2000
3M's internal toxicology research, conducted over two decades, finds PFOS in the blood of the general public — people with no occupational exposure. The compound had been building in human bodies globally without anyone outside the company knowing. Under EPA pressure, 3M voluntarily phases out PFOS production beginning in 2000. Internal documents later show the company had identified the contamination problem years earlier.
2001
Attorney Robert Bilott, working on behalf of a West Virginia farmer, files what becomes a class-action lawsuit against DuPont on behalf of roughly 70,000 residents near the company's Washington Works plant in Parkersburg, W.Va. Discovery produces decades of internal DuPont research showing the company knew PFOA caused cancer in laboratory animals and had detected it in the blood of workers and their families. The case eventually compels the creation of the C8 Health Project, an independent epidemiological study.
2006–2011
The C8 Science Panel — three independent epidemiologists appointed as part of the DuPont settlement — analyzes health data from the 70,000 enrolled Parkersburg-area residents and establishes "probable links" between PFOA exposure and six diseases: kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, ulcerative colitis, hypercholesterolemia (high cholesterol), and pregnancy-induced hypertension. The findings provide the strongest epidemiological basis to date for PFAS health regulation.
2016
EPA issues a lifetime health advisory for PFOA and PFOS at 70 parts per trillion in drinking water. Health advisories are not enforceable limits — they carry no legal weight and impose no obligation on water systems. But the advisory is the first federal threshold for any PFAS compound and signals that EPA has concluded the chemicals pose a health risk at concentrations previously considered negligible.
2017–2021 (Trump 1)
The first Trump EPA releases the PFAS Action Plan in February 2019, committing to begin the rulemaking process for binding drinking water limits. It adds 172 PFAS to the Toxics Release Inventory in June 2020. It issues a significant new use rule requiring EPA review before long-chain PFAS can reach the market. On January 15, 2021 — five days before leaving office — it publishes an advance notice of proposed rulemaking on designating PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances. No binding limits are finalized before the administration ends.
2021–2024 (Biden)
The Biden EPA completes the PFAS regulatory agenda initiated over the previous two decades. In 2023 it finalizes the TSCA Section 8(a)(7) reporting rule, requiring all manufacturers and importers to disclose PFAS use — the most comprehensive PFAS data collection in U.S. history. In May 2024 it designates PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances, the first synthetic chemicals designated since the law's enactment in 1980. In April 2024 it finalizes the first binding drinking water limits: 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS, plus limits for four additional compounds.
2025–present (Trump 2)
The second Trump EPA rescinds drinking water protections for four of the six regulated PFAS — GenX, PFHxS, PFNA, and PFBS — in May 2025, citing cost concerns and arguing the scientific record was insufficient to support those specific limits. The 4 ppt limits for PFOA and PFOS survive, but only after federal courts deny EPA's requests to delay compliance deadlines. In a public contradiction, the MAHA Commission — the administration's own chronic-disease advisory body — names PFAS a driver of childhood illness in a June 2025 report published roughly two weeks after the drinking water rescissions.

Where PFAS are used

The following categories account for the largest known uses of PFAS in the United States. Many consumer products no longer use the longest-chain compounds — PFOA and PFOS — but have substituted shorter-chain PFAS whose long-term health effects are less well studied.

  • Nonstick cookware — PTFE (Teflon) coatings on pans, baking sheets, waffle irons, and small appliances. DuPont phased out PFOA as a processing aid in PTFE manufacturing by 2013 under an EPA voluntary agreement, but some shorter-chain PFAS remain in use in alternative nonstick coatings marketed as "PFOA-free."
  • Food packaging — Grease-resistant coatings on fast-food wrappers, pizza boxes, microwave popcorn bags, and paper takeout containers. The FDA banned certain PFAS from food-contact paper and paperboard in 2021, but enforcement and transition timelines are ongoing. Compostable packaging that still uses PFAS can transfer the compounds to food and, eventually, to composted soil.
  • Firefighting foam (AFFF) — Aqueous film-forming foam, used at military installations, civilian airports, oil refineries, and industrial facilities to suppress fuel fires. AFFF is one of the most significant known contamination pathways in the United States: PFAS from foam training and emergency use has leached into groundwater near hundreds of military bases. The Department of Defense has been transitioning away from AFFF, but cleanup of existing contamination will take decades.
  • Stain- and water-resistant textiles — Scotchgard treatments for carpets, furniture upholstery, and clothing. PFAS-based durable water repellents (DWR) have been standard on outdoor gear, rain jackets, ski wear, and work uniforms. Gore-Tex and similar "waterproof breathable" membranes historically relied on PFAS chemistry. Several outdoor apparel brands have committed to PFAS-free alternatives, though performance trade-offs remain an industry debate.
  • Cosmetics — "Long-lasting," "waterproof," and "transfer-resistant" cosmetics — mascara, foundation, lipstick, blush, eyeliner — frequently contain PFAS as film-formers, conditioning agents, or texture modifiers. The FDA does not require PFAS disclosure on cosmetic labels; studies have detected PFAS in products whose ingredient lists contain no explicit PFAS names.
  • Medical devices — PTFE is biocompatible, chemically inert, and used in vascular grafts, surgical mesh, catheter coatings, suture materials, and implantable devices. Medical applications are among the uses most commonly cited as difficult to replace without performance loss.
  • Semiconductor and electronics manufacturing — PFAS are used as process chemicals in chip fabrication (photolithography, etching, and cleaning steps), circuit board manufacturing, and flat-panel display production. The semiconductor industry has sought PFAS exemptions from European restriction proposals, arguing no suitable substitutes currently exist at commercial scale.
  • Industrial lubricants and coatings — Aerospace components, automotive parts, industrial bearings, and heavy equipment use PFAS-based coatings for low friction, chemical resistance, and high-temperature stability. Applications range from aircraft hydraulic systems to medical implant surfaces.
