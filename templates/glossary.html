Glossary

Key terms, compounds, and programs in U.S. PFAS regulation.

{# ── PFAS Compounds ──────────────────────────────────────────────── #}

PFAS Compounds

  • Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances PFAS

    A family of roughly 12,000 synthetic chemicals that share a carbon-fluorine bond — one of the strongest in chemistry. Because this bond resists breakdown by heat, water, oil, and biological processes, PFAS accumulate in the environment and in living organisms indefinitely. They are often called "forever chemicals."

  • Perfluorooctanoic acid PFOA

    An 8-carbon PFAS used in the manufacture of nonstick coatings (including Teflon) and other fluoropolymers. One of the most studied PFAS compounds. Linked to kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, and immune system disruption. Subject to the Biden EPA's 2024 4 ppt maximum contaminant level, retained by Trump 2.

  • Perfluorooctane sulfonate PFOS

    An 8-carbon PFAS historically used in stain-resistant fabric treatments (Scotchgard) and aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) used by military and civilian firefighters. Phased out by major manufacturers in the early 2000s but persists widely in groundwater near military bases and industrial sites. Subject to the same 4 ppt MCL as PFOA.

  • GenX HFPO-DA

    Hexafluoropropylene oxide-dimer acid — a short-chain PFAS developed as a replacement for PFOA in fluoropolymer manufacturing. Associated with the Chemours facility on the Cape Fear River in North Carolina, which discharged GenX into drinking water supplies for communities downstream including Wilmington, NC. Subject to a Biden-era MCL that was rescinded by Trump 2 in May 2025.

  • Perfluorohexane sulfonate PFHxS

    A 6-carbon PFAS found in firefighting foam and various consumer products. Linked to thyroid disease and developmental effects. Subject to a Biden-era MCL rescinded by Trump 2 in May 2025.

  • Perfluorononanoic acid PFNA

    A 9-carbon PFAS found in food packaging, stain-resistant coatings, and some industrial applications. Linked to immune suppression, thyroid effects, and obesity. EPA scientists reportedly flagged PFNA as more toxic than previously classified before its MCL was rescinded in May 2025.

  • Perfluorobutane sulfonate PFBS

    A short-chain (4-carbon) PFAS used as a replacement for PFOS in some applications. Previously considered less toxic because it clears the body faster, but newer research has raised concerns about thyroid and developmental effects. MCL rescinded by Trump 2 in May 2025.

{# ── Regulatory Standards ────────────────────────────────────────── #}

Regulatory Standards & Programs

  • Maximum Contaminant Level MCL

    The legally enforceable limit on the concentration of a contaminant in public drinking water systems, set under the Safe Drinking Water Act. Water systems that exceed an MCL must notify customers and take corrective action. Biden's April 2024 MCL final rule set the first-ever binding PFAS limits: 4 ppt for PFOA and PFOS, and limits for four other compounds. Trump 2 retained the PFOA/PFOS limits and rescinded the other four.

    Also see: MCLG (Maximum Contaminant Level Goal), a non-enforceable health-based target. For PFOA and PFOS, the MCLG is zero — meaning no level is considered safe.

  • Safe Drinking Water Act SDWA

    The federal law governing the safety of public drinking water systems in the United States. Enacted in 1974. Requires EPA to set MCLs for contaminants that pose a risk to public health. The SDWA's anti-backsliding provision may limit EPA's authority to rescind finalized MCLs — a legal question raised in litigation challenging the Trump 2 rollback.

  • Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act CERCLA

    The federal Superfund law, enacted in 1980. Designating a substance as a CERCLA hazardous substance gives EPA authority to compel cleanup of contaminated sites and hold polluters liable for remediation costs. Biden designated PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances in May 2024 — the most powerful PFAS enforcement tool available. Trump 2 has retained the designation.

  • Toxic Substances Control Act TSCA

    The federal law governing the manufacture, use, and disposal of industrial chemicals in the United States. Key TSCA provisions used in PFAS regulation include: Section 8(a)(7) — reporting requirements; Section 5 — new chemical review (used to require EPA approval before new PFAS enter the market, later subject to a 5th Circuit ruling limiting EPA's authority); and Section 6 — risk management rules.

  • TSCA Section 8(a)(7) Reporting Rule

    A Biden-era rule requiring companies to report to EPA all PFAS they manufacture, process, import, or use — including the quantities and uses. Described as the most comprehensive federal PFAS data collection ever conducted. Trump 2 proposed significant exemptions in November 2025, including a de minimis exemption (below 0.1% concentration) and an imported articles exemption.

  • Toxics Release Inventory TRI

    A publicly available database maintained by EPA listing toxic chemical releases, transfers, and disposal from industrial facilities. The first Trump administration added 172 PFAS to the TRI in 2019 as part of the PFAS Action Plan — one of its most tangible accomplishments.

  • Unregulated Contaminant Monitoring Rule 5 UCMR5

    A Biden-era rule requiring public water systems to monitor for 29 PFAS compounds and lithium between 2023 and 2025. Results confirmed PFAS above the new MCL levels in systems serving approximately 70 million Americans — providing the data baseline for the 2024 drinking water rule.

  • Significant New Use Rule SNUR

    A TSCA Section 5 regulatory tool requiring companies to notify EPA before using a chemical in a new way that could increase exposure or risk. EPA used SNURs against PFAS manufacturing processes; however, a March 2024 5th Circuit ruling (Inhance Technologies v. EPA) found that EPA had exceeded its authority in issuing certain PFAS SNURs — limiting this enforcement tool.

{# ── Key Laws & Concepts ─────────────────────────────────────────── #}

Key Laws, Concepts & Abbreviations

  • Parts per trillion ppt

    The unit used to measure PFAS concentrations in water. One part per trillion is equivalent to one drop of water in 20 Olympic-size swimming pools. The Biden EPA's MCL for PFOA and PFOS is 4 ppt — an extremely low threshold reflecting the compounds' high bioaccumulation and their health risks even at trace concentrations.

  • Aqueous film-forming foam AFFF

    A PFAS-containing firefighting foam historically used at military bases, airports, and refineries to suppress fuel fires. AFFF training exercises and spills have contaminated groundwater near hundreds of military installations, making the Department of Defense one of the largest known sources of PFAS contamination in the U.S.

  • Make America Healthy Again MAHA

    A public health initiative associated with HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. The MAHA Commission released a report in June 2025 naming PFAS, pesticides, and food additives as contributors to chronic disease in American children. The report's PFAS concerns were published two weeks after EPA rescinded MCLs for four PFAS compounds, creating a visible contradiction within the administration.

  • Bioaccumulation

    The process by which a substance builds up in an organism's tissue faster than the organism can eliminate it. PFAS bioaccumulate because the body cannot break them down. Exposure is cumulative over a lifetime. Blood-serum PFAS levels reflect total lifetime exposure, not just recent contact.

  • Anti-backsliding provision

    A provision of the Safe Drinking Water Act (42 U.S.C. § 300g-1(b)(9)) that prohibits EPA from revising a national primary drinking water regulation in a way that results in a less stringent MCL. Earthjustice and state attorneys general have argued this provision makes Trump 2's MCL rescissions unlawful. Courts have not yet ruled on the merits.

  • PFAS Action Plan

    Released in February 2019 by the first Trump administration under EPA Administrator Andrew Wheeler, the PFAS Action Plan was the first comprehensive federal strategy for addressing PFAS contamination. It committed EPA to developing MCLs, expanding TRI reporting, and pursuing CERCLA designation — laying groundwork that Biden's EPA completed.

  • Enforcement discretion

    A decision by a regulatory agency not to enforce a rule against a particular category of regulated parties, even when violations technically exist. Trump 2's September 2025 CERCLA announcement retained the PFOA/PFOS designation but granted enforcement discretion to municipalities, water utilities, farmers, and fire departments — while continuing enforcement against manufacturers and industrial polluters.

  • De minimis exemption

    A regulatory exemption for quantities below a defined threshold, on the theory that the amount is too small to regulate effectively. The Trump 2 proposed TSCA reporting exemptions would create a de minimis exemption for PFAS below 0.1% concentration — a threshold that would cover a wide range of products, including many consumer goods that contain PFAS in small but potentially significant amounts.
