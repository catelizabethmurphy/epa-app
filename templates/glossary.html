Frequently asked questions

What PFAS are, why they matter, what the government did about them, and key terms used in this tracker.

{# ── THE BASICS ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── #}

The chemicals

What are PFAS?

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances are a family of roughly 12,000 synthetic chemicals united by a single structural feature: a chain of carbon atoms bonded to fluorine. The carbon-fluorine bond is the strongest in organic chemistry — no enzyme, bacterium, or amount of heat breaks it. That permanence was engineered deliberately. It turned out to be their defining danger.

PFAS repel water, oil, and heat. They make surfaces nonstick, fabrics waterproof, and foam capable of smothering fuel fires. Those properties made them commercially irresistible. From the 1950s onward they spread into cookware, food packaging, carpets, clothing, firefighting foam, cosmetics, and semiconductor manufacturing.

Why are they called "forever chemicals"?

Because they don't break down. Not in the environment. Not in your body. The carbon-fluorine bond resists every biological and chemical process that would degrade most compounds. PFAS accumulate in soil, drift through groundwater, and build up in human tissue over a lifetime of exposure — which is why blood-serum levels reflect total lifetime exposure, not just recent contact.

They are now found in Arctic ice, deep-ocean fish, and human breast milk on every inhabited continent.

Where are PFAS found?

Virtually everywhere consumer and industrial products have been used for the past 70 years:

  • Nonstick cookware — PTFE (Teflon) coatings. DuPont phased out PFOA as a processing aid by 2013, but shorter-chain PFAS remain in many "PFOA-free" alternatives.
  • Food packaging — grease-resistant coatings on fast-food wrappers, pizza boxes, microwave popcorn bags, and paper takeout containers.
  • Firefighting foam (AFFF) — aqueous film-forming foam used at military bases, airports, and refineries. One of the most significant contamination pathways in the U.S.; PFAS from foam training has leached into groundwater near hundreds of installations.
  • Stain- and water-resistant textiles — Scotchgard treatments, outdoor gear, rain jackets, ski wear, and work uniforms.
  • Cosmetics — mascara, foundation, and lipstick marketed as "long-lasting" or "waterproof" frequently contain PFAS as film-formers; the FDA does not require disclosure.
  • Semiconductor manufacturing — process chemicals in chip fabrication, for which the industry has argued no substitutes exist at commercial scale.
What health effects are linked to PFAS?

In 2006–2011, an independent epidemiological panel analyzing data from 70,000 residents near DuPont's West Virginia plant established "probable links" between PFOA exposure and six diseases: kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, ulcerative colitis, high cholesterol, and pregnancy-induced hypertension.

Subsequent research has added immune suppression, low infant birth weight, and developmental delays in children to the list of PFAS-associated health effects. In 2022, the National Academies of Sciences concluded the evidence of harm was sufficient to act on without waiting for definitive causal proof for every compound.

The CDC has detected PFAS in the blood of nearly every American it has tested. In June 2025, the White House's own MAHA Commission named PFAS a key driver of childhood chronic illness — two weeks after the Trump EPA rescinded drinking water protections for four PFAS compounds.

How did PFAS get into public drinking water?

Multiple pathways — but firefighting foam is among the largest known sources. AFFF used in training exercises at military installations and civilian airports for decades leached into the soil and groundwater beneath those sites. PFAS from industrial manufacturing plants (particularly DuPont's Washington Works facility on the Ohio River and Chemours's Cape Fear River facility in North Carolina) discharged directly into surface water used for drinking water intake. Food packaging and consumer products shed PFAS into landfill leachate, which can contaminate nearby groundwater.

A 2023–2025 EPA monitoring program (UCMR5) found PFAS above the new federal limits in water systems serving approximately 70 million Americans.

{# ── THE RULES ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── #}

The regulations

What is an MCL, and why does it matter?

An MCL — Maximum Contaminant Level — is the legally enforceable limit on the concentration of a contaminant in public drinking water, set under the Safe Drinking Water Act. Water systems that exceed an MCL must notify customers and take corrective action.

In April 2024, the Biden EPA finalized the first-ever binding federal MCLs for PFAS: 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS, and limits for four other compounds. Water utilities were given five years to comply. The rule was estimated to protect 100 million Americans.

In May 2025, the Trump EPA rescinded the MCLs for four of those six compounds — PFNA, PFHxS, HFPO-DA (GenX), and mixtures — citing cost and feasibility. The PFOA and PFOS limits at 4 ppt were retained.

Note: 4 parts per trillion is roughly equivalent to four drops in an Olympic-size swimming pool. The MCLG — the non-enforceable health-based goal — for PFOA and PFOS is zero, meaning no level is considered safe.

What is CERCLA, and what does "hazardous substance designation" mean?

CERCLA — the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, known as Superfund — is the federal law enacted in 1980 that gives EPA authority to compel cleanup of contaminated sites and hold polluters financially liable for remediation costs.

Designating a chemical as a "hazardous substance" under CERCLA is the legal trigger that creates that liability. In April 2024, the Biden administration designated PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances — the first new designations in over two decades. Companies that released these chemicals can now be held responsible for cleanup costs at the roughly 700 federal facilities with known or suspected PFAS contamination.

The Trump II administration retained the designation but slowed enforcement — exempting municipalities, water utilities, farmers, and fire departments while continuing enforcement against manufacturers.

Can EPA legally rescind a finalized MCL?

That is actively disputed. The Safe Drinking Water Act contains an anti-backsliding provision (42 U.S.C. § 300g-1(b)(9)) that prohibits EPA from revising a national primary drinking water regulation in a way that results in a less stringent standard. Earthjustice and a coalition of state attorneys general have argued this provision makes the Trump II rescissions unlawful.

Courts have not yet ruled on the merits of the challenge as of this writing.

What other federal tools govern PFAS?
  • TSCA (Toxic Substances Control Act) — governs manufacture, use, and disposal of industrial chemicals. The Biden EPA issued a Section 8(a)(7) rule requiring all PFAS manufacturers and importers to disclose PFAS use — the most comprehensive federal PFAS data collection in U.S. history. Trump II proposed significant exemptions in November 2025, including a de minimis exemption for concentrations below 0.1%.
  • Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) — the first Trump administration added 172 PFAS to this public database of toxic releases in 2019, one of its most tangible PFAS actions.
  • UCMR5 — a Biden-era rule requiring water systems to monitor for 29 PFAS compounds between 2023 and 2025, producing the data baseline used to justify the 2024 MCL rule.
{# ── THE RECORD ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── #}

The policy record

What did the first Trump administration do?

The first Trump EPA was the first federal administration to formally acknowledge PFAS as a national public health problem. In February 2019 it released a PFAS Action Plan — a framework committing to set MCLs for PFOA and PFOS. In June 2020 it added 172 PFAS to the Toxics Release Inventory. On January 15, 2021 — five days before leaving office — it published an advance notice of proposed rulemaking on CERCLA designation.

None of the binding commitments were finalized before the administration ended. States, military communities, and manufacturers like 3M and DuPont were already in litigation. The federal government had acknowledged the problem; it had not yet acted on it.

What did the Biden administration do?

The Biden EPA completed the regulatory agenda the prior administration had begun. Key actions:

  • 2021 — Released a PFAS Strategic Roadmap committing to enforceable standards within two years
  • 2023 — Proposed the first federal PFAS drinking water standards; finalized the TSCA Section 8(a)(7) reporting rule
  • April 2024 — Finalized MCLs for six PFAS compounds (4 ppt for PFOA and PFOS; limits for four additional compounds)
  • April 2024 — Designated PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances — the first new Superfund designation in over 20 years

Water utilities were given five years to comply with the MCLs. The rule covered systems serving at least 25 people.

What has the second Trump administration done?

In May 2025, the Trump EPA rescinded MCLs for four of the six regulated PFAS — PFNA, PFHxS, HFPO-DA (GenX), and mixtures — citing cost and feasibility concerns. The limits for PFOA and PFOS at 4 ppt were retained; the compliance deadline for those two was extended after federal courts denied EPA's request to pause them.

The Superfund designation for PFOA and PFOS was retained, but enforcement shifted — exempting municipalities, utilities, farmers, and fire departments while maintaining liability for manufacturers.

In June 2025 — two weeks after the rescissions — the White House's MAHA Commission published a report naming PFAS a key driver of childhood chronic illness. The administration did not address the contradiction.

{# ── KEY TERMS ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── #}

Key terms and compounds

What are PFOA and PFOS?

PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid) is an 8-carbon PFAS used in manufacturing nonstick coatings including Teflon. Linked to kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, and immune disruption. DuPont's use of PFOA and its decades of documented internal knowledge of its harms became the basis for major litigation and the eventual regulatory action.

PFOS (perfluorooctane sulfonate) is an 8-carbon PFAS used in Scotchgard fabric treatments and firefighting foam. 3M phased it out under EPA pressure in 2000 after its own internal research found it in the blood of the general public. Both compounds are now subject to the 4 ppt MCL retained by Trump II, and both are designated CERCLA hazardous substances.

What is GenX?

GenX is the commercial name for HFPO-DA (hexafluoropropylene oxide-dimer acid), a short-chain PFAS developed by Chemours (a DuPont spinoff) as a replacement for PFOA in fluoropolymer manufacturing. It came to national attention after it was detected in the drinking water of communities downstream from Chemours's Fayetteville Works facility on the Cape Fear River in North Carolina — including Wilmington, NC.

GenX was subject to a Biden-era MCL of 10 ppt, which the Trump EPA rescinded in May 2025.

What is AFFF?

Aqueous film-forming foam — a PFAS-containing firefighting agent used at military bases, civilian airports, and oil refineries to suppress fuel fires. AFFF training exercises and accidental spills have contaminated groundwater near hundreds of military installations, making the Department of Defense one of the largest known sources of PFAS contamination in the United States. DoD has been transitioning away from AFFF, but cleanup of existing contamination will take decades.

What does "parts per trillion" mean?

Parts per trillion (ppt) is the unit used to measure PFAS concentrations in water. One part per trillion is equivalent to one drop of water in 20 Olympic-size swimming pools. The Biden MCL for PFOA and PFOS — 4 ppt — reflects the compounds' potency: they accumulate in human tissue and pose health risks at concentrations that were previously considered negligible and were technically difficult to even detect.

What is MAHA?

Make America Healthy Again — a public health initiative associated with HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. The MAHA Commission released a report in June 2025 identifying PFAS, pesticides, and food additives as drivers of chronic disease in American children. The report was published approximately two weeks after the Trump EPA rescinded drinking water protections for four PFAS compounds, creating a visible contradiction within the administration.
