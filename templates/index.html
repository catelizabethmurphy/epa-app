The contamination

They're in your tap water, your blood, and very likely your kitchen pan.

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — PFAS — are a class of roughly 12,000 synthetic chemicals manufactured since the 1930s and designed, above all else, to not break down. They coat nonstick cookware, line food packaging, waterproof rain jackets, and saturate the firefighting foam sprayed at military bases and airports for decades. Because they don't degrade, they accumulate — in soil, in groundwater, in the human body. The CDC has found them in the bloodstream of 97 percent of Americans tested.

The scale

An estimated 176 million Americans drink tap water with detectable PFAS levels.

These chemicals don't stay where they're made. They wash from factory floors into rivers, sink into aquifers, travel thousands of miles through the atmosphere. They've been detected in Arctic ice cores, in fish from remote mountain lakes, in human breast milk on every inhabited continent. There is no PFAS-free corner of the planet. Scientists have called it a global contamination crisis. The water systems serving roughly half the U.S. population exceed what EPA now considers a safe level.

The evidence

A cattle farmer's lawsuit in West Virginia produced the largest PFAS health study ever conducted.

In the 1990s, Wilbur Tennant noticed his cattle dying near a DuPont landfill outside Parkersburg, W.Va. Attorney Rob Bilott took the case, and DuPont's own suppressed research began to surface. A 2004 settlement required the company to fund the C8 Science Panel — a six-year study of 69,000 people living near the plant. Its findings linked PFOA to kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, high cholesterol, and pregnancy complications. That single community study reshaped PFAS science worldwide and gave regulators their first durable evidence base for action.

April 2024

After 80 years of contamination, the first legally binding federal limits — ever.

The Biden EPA finalized maximum contaminant levels for six PFAS compounds in drinking water: 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS, with additional limits for GenX, PFHxS, PFNA, and PFBS. It was the first time the federal government had set an enforceable ceiling on any PFAS in any medium. Public water systems had until 2029 to comply. The chemical industry and water utilities challenged the rule in the D.C. Circuit within weeks of its publication.

May 2025

The same administration whose health agenda names PFAS a disease driver rolled back four of those six limits.

In May 2025, the Trump EPA announced it would rescind the drinking water standards for GenX, PFHxS, PFNA, and PFBS. The 4 ppt limits for PFOA and PFOS survived only because federal courts blocked EPA's attempts to delay compliance. This happened two weeks before the MAHA Commission — the administration's own chronic-disease task force — published a report naming PFAS a driver of the childhood health crisis it was formed to address. The contradiction sits at the center of this tracker.

Internal contradiction

What the MAHA agenda says vs. what EPA is doing

MAHA position

The Make America Healthy Again agenda, led by HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., frames PFAS as a driver of the chronic-disease epidemic — linking these compounds to cancer, thyroid disruption, immune suppression, and developmental harm.

The MAHA Commission's June 2025 report explicitly named PFAS as a contributor to childhood chronic disease. It was published roughly two weeks after EPA announced it would rescind drinking water limits for four PFAS compounds.

EPA's regulatory record

  • Day 2: Withdrew effluent limits requiring PFAS discharge controls from industrial facilities
  • May 2025: Rescinded drinking water limits for GenX, PFHxS, PFNA, PFBS
  • Extended: PFOA/PFOS compliance deadline pushed from 2029 to 2031
  • Nov. 2025: Proposed exemptions reducing TSCA industry disclosure requirement
  • Retained: 4 ppt limits for PFOA and PFOS (upheld under court pressure)
  • Retained: CERCLA hazardous substance designation for PFOA/PFOS
