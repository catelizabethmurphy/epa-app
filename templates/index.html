The chemicals

They don't break down. Not in the environment. Not in your body.

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances are a family of more than 12,000 synthetic compounds built around one of the strongest bonds in organic chemistry: carbon and fluorine. That bond is what makes them useful — and what makes them nearly impossible to destroy. Scientists call them "forever chemicals."

They are in nonstick cookware, food packaging, stain-resistant fabric, and the firefighting foam used at airports and military bases for decades. They leach into groundwater and accumulate in human tissue over a lifetime.

Long-term exposure is linked to kidney and testicular cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, and developmental delays in children. The CDC has found PFAS in the blood of nearly every American it has tested.

{# ══ CHAPTER: TRUMP I ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ #}
Trump I
2017–2021

Recognizing the problem

The first administration to formally acknowledge PFAS as a national crisis — but stopped short of binding rules.

In 2018, as evidence of widespread contamination mounted, the EPA announced a PFAS national leadership summit. In 2019 it released a PFAS Action Plan — a framework committing to set maximum contaminant levels for PFOA and PFOS in drinking water. But the administration never finalized those limits. It also proposed designating PFOA and PFOS as Superfund hazardous substances, which would make polluters pay for cleanup. That proposal, too, was never finalized.

States, military communities, and manufacturers like 3M and DuPont were already in litigation. The federal government had acknowledged the problem. It had not yet acted on it.

{# ══ CHAPTER: BIDEN ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ #}
Biden
2021–2025

Setting standards

For the first time, EPA set enforceable limits — on drinking water and on who pays for cleanup.

In April 2024, the Biden EPA finalized the first federal drinking water limits for PFAS — called Maximum Contaminant Levels, or MCLs. Six compounds were capped at concentrations so low they approach the threshold of laboratory detection. Water utilities were given five years to comply. The rule was estimated to protect 100 million Americans.

That same month, PFOA and PFOS became the first chemicals added to the Superfund hazardous substances list in over two decades — making companies legally responsible for cleaning up sites they contaminated. The designation covers roughly 700 federal facilities with known or suspected PFAS contamination.

{# ══ CHAPTER: TRUMP II ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════ #}
Trump II
2025–present

Reversing course ... some of the time

One year after the drinking water rule took effect, the EPA rescinded four of its six limits.

In May 2025, the Trump EPA withdrew the MCLs for PFNA, PFHxS, HFPO-DA (GenX), and mixtures — citing cost and feasibility. The two limits it kept — for PFOA and PFOS — remained at the same level Biden set: 4 parts per trillion. The compliance deadline for those two was extended. Enforcement of the Superfund designation slowed.
