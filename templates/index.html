What are PFAS?

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — a family of roughly 12,000 synthetic chemicals first developed in the 1940s for nonstick pans, firefighting foam, waterproof gear, and food packaging. The carbon-fluorine bond is among the strongest in chemistry. Nothing in nature breaks them down. They persist in soil, water, and body tissue indefinitely.

Why they're dangerous

Studies link PFAS exposure to kidney and testicular cancer, thyroid hormone disruption, weakened immune response (including reduced vaccine effectiveness), and developmental harm in fetuses and infants. The compounds bioaccumulate — building up in tissue over a lifetime. Children, who drink more water relative to body weight, face the highest risk.

The MAHA contradiction

The Make America Healthy Again agenda, led by HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., explicitly names PFAS as a chronic-disease driver and demands strict regulation. The Trump EPA's simultaneous rollback of binding PFAS drinking water limits creates a direct contradiction within the administration — one this tracker documents.

MAHA Tension

What the MAHA agenda says vs. what EPA is doing

MAHA position

The Make America Healthy Again coalition, led by HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., frames PFAS as a driver of the chronic-disease epidemic — linking these compounds to cancer, thyroid disruption, immune suppression, and developmental problems in children.

The MAHA Commission's June 2025 report explicitly named PFAS contamination alongside pesticides and food dyes as contributors to childhood chronic disease. It was released roughly two weeks after EPA announced it would rescind MCLs for four PFAS compounds.

EPA's regulatory record

  • Day 2: Withdrew effluent limits requiring PFAS discharge controls from industrial facilities
  • Rolled back: MCLs for GenX, PFHxS, PFNA, PFBS rescinded (May 2025)
  • Weakened: PFOA/PFOS compliance deadline extended 2029 → 2031
  • Proposed: TSCA reporting exemptions would reduce required industry disclosure
  • Retained: 4 ppt MCLs for PFOA and PFOS (upheld under court pressure)
  • Retained: CERCLA hazardous substance designation for PFOA/PFOS

The arc of federal PFAS regulation

Trump 1 started the regulatory process. Biden completed it. Trump 2 is unwinding the results. Source: EPA Jan. 19, 2021 →

Trump 1 (2017–2021)
Building the framework
  • Released first-ever PFAS Action Plan (Feb. 2019)
  • Added 172 PFAS to Toxic Release Inventory
  • New TSCA rule: PFAS can't reach market without EPA review
  • Formally initiated MCL development for PFOA/PFOS
  • Began CERCLA hazardous substance evaluation
  • Proposed UCMR5: nationwide PFAS testing in water systems
  • Awarded $15M+ in PFAS research grants
  • 16 PFAS enforcement actions

Critical gap: EPA started the MCL process but did not complete it.

Biden (2021–2025)
Completing the rules
  • April 2024: First binding MCLs for 6 PFAS (4 ppt PFOA/PFOS)
  • May 2024: PFOA/PFOS designated CERCLA hazardous substances
  • UCMR5 confirmed PFAS in water for ~70M Americans
  • TSCA Section 8(a)(7): mandatory PFAS reporting by industry
Trump 2 (2025–)
Unwinding the results
  • Day 2: Withdrew industrial PFAS discharge limits (ELG rule)
  • May 2025: Rescinded MCLs for GenX, PFHxS, PFNA, PFBS
  • Extended PFOA/PFOS compliance: 2029 → 2031
  • Nov 2025: Proposed exemptions to TSCA reporting rule
  • Retained PFOA/PFOS MCLs (4 ppt)
  • Retained CERCLA designation (pledged to "hold polluters accountable")
  • Launched PFAS-OUT initiative (non-binding, guidance only)
